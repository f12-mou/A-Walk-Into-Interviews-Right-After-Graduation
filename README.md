If you are one of them, then this is for you.

First of all, warm congratulations to you. You are very close to completing a major milestone in your life, yoohooo. Graduation is not just an endpoint. It is the beginning of a completely new phase. What comes next is both exciting and overwhelming.

Your days were filled with 8 AM to 5 PM classes, campus life, deadlines, and spontaneous get-togethers with friends. Slowly, these will turn into memories. In their place comes a new reality—professional life, responsibilities, independence, and decisions that actually shape your future.

I remember being at this exact stage and feeling quite overwhelmed.

A lot of questions start to appear:
- What will life be like after graduation?
- Where do I even begin?
- How do I prepare for interviews?
- Am I already too late or not good enough?


Uncertainty is completely normal at this point. So before anything else, take a deep breath. I want to walk you through this phase—how to approach interview preparation, how to think about academia vs industry, and how to move forward. 


## Job Prospects for CSE Graduates

There are a wide range of opportunities available for CSE graduates. You might consider roles in industry, academia, remote jobs, research positions, or even pursue higher studies. The options are diverse, and there is no single “correct” path.

In this guide, I will primarily focus on two major directions:
- **Industry**
- **Academia**

This is because I personally prepared for these two paths and maybe can share practical insights from that experience.

A very common question at this stage is:

> Should I prepare for one specific path, or keep multiple options open?

The answer is: **it completely depends on you**.

It depends on:
- how much pressure you can handle  
- how well you can manage time  
- how clear you are about your long-term goals  

There are indeed some overlapping areas between academia and industry preparation. However, when it comes to interviews, these two paths are quite different.

Before diving into preparation strategies, it is important to understand:

> What should you keep ready *before* you even get an interview call or apply for some position?

In the next section, we will go through the essential things you should prepare, organize, and document in advance.

## Preparation Checklist Before Interviews

Before you start applying or receiving interview calls, it is important to have certain materials ready. These represent you even before you speak.

---

<details>
<summary><b>1. Curriculum Vitae (CV)</b></summary>

There is no hard and fast rule for writing a CV. I am sharing the points that I used to follow or learned from seniors.

1. A standard CV is usually around 2 pages. Even some companies explicitly prefer this. However, if you genuinely have content to show, you may extend it. My own CV was 3.5 pages long. The CV should be in a single column. CVs that are automatically parsed may face issues with having two columns. Background color must be white. What matters is clarity and conciseness.

2. It is generally recommended **not to include your picture** in the CV.

3. The header section should contain your basic information, such as your full name, email address, phone number, GitHub link, LinkedIn profile, and portfolio link. For academia-focused CVs, avoid adding LinkedIn.

4. You should list your education in reverse chronological order. Start with your university, including duration, CGPA, and class rank if available. Then add your college and high school. There is no need to include primary school xD (I have actually seen some CVs with that).

5. If it is an academia CV, you may add a research interest section next. Do not list too many areas. Keep it aligned with your thesis work and maybe one or two related fields.

6. After that, you can include your research experience or thesis section. As an undergraduate, this is often your thesis or a milestone project. Keep this section separate, as both academia and entry-level industry roles value it highly. You can mention the title, your supervisor’s name, a short summary including the problem definition and specifically what contribution you made to solve it, and the current status (such as submitted or under review). If it is being drafted, no worries. It is quite normal. If you have publications, you may list them here. However, this list should be meaningful. A longer list without strong contributions, or just arXiv uploads, does not add much value. Interviewers are experienced and understand the depth of work.

7. Next comes your achievements section. Keep this concise and relevant. Extracurricular activities are good, but they do not add much value unless they are relevant to the role. For example, I had awards in Rabindrasangeet competitions, but I would not include them here. On the other hand, achievements in programming contests, hackathons, datathons, or CTFs demonstrate technical strength.

8. Then comes your projects section. You can include well-documented and recognizable projects from your undergraduate life. Sometimes you may wonder whether a project is meaningful enough. Even if you implemented an existing algorithm or system as part of coursework, it still carries value. For example, I was once asked about implementing a feedforward neural network. Since I had built it from scratch, I could explain it in detail, and they appreciated that. The work itself was not novel, but understanding and implementing something deeply is very valuable.

9. After that, you can include remaining sections such as technical skills, extracurricular projects, and problem-solving experience. You may include links or stats from platforms like Codeforces or LeetCode, even if you are not a regular contestant. This is relevant for both industry and academia. I was asked in a university interview about my problem-solving experience, including how many problems I solved and how I balanced it with coursework.

10. Finally, you may include one or two references.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>2. LinkedIn Profile</b></summary>

LinkedIn is often overlooked, but it plays an important role—especially for industry roles.

Recruiters often check LinkedIn before or after shortlisting candidates.

Make sure your profile includes:
- a clear headline (not just “Student”)  
- updated education and experience  
- projects and skills  
- consistent information with your CV  

You do not need to be extremely active, but your profile should be **complete and clean**.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary><b>3. GitHub Profile</b></summary>

A well-organized GitHub profile reflects:
- your coding habits  
- consistency  
- project depth  

Try to:
- pin your best repositories  
- write proper README files  
- avoid uploading random/unstructured code  

Quality matters much more than quantity.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary><b>4. Project Documentation</b></summary>

Each project should be understandable to someone who has never seen it before.

At minimum, include:
- problem description  
- approach  
- technologies used  
- results (if any)  

Good documentation often makes an average project stand out.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary><b>5. Thesis / Milestone Project Documentation</b></summary>

This is one of the most important parts for both academia and industry.

You should be able to clearly explain:
- problem statement  
- motivation  
- methodology  
- results  
- your contribution  

Interviewers often go deep into this.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary><b>6. Portfolio (Optional)</b></summary>

A portfolio helps you present everything in one place.

It is especially useful for:
- web development  
- machine learning projects  
- design-heavy work  

If you have one, it can significantly improve your presentation.

</details>
